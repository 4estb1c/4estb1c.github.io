late lunch: within 5 min of the hotel
Hadongkwan (Myeongdong gomtang) ~$13
Since 1939, one dish only: a milky beef-bone broth loaded with brisket, served with rice and kimchi you salt yourself. Enormous protein hit, zero decisions, and the best possible first bowl in Korea. Closes 4pm.

The stalls set up along the main pedestrian streets through the afternoon and go full carnival by dusk: cheese corn dogs, tornado potato, grilled cheese-and-corn cups, egg bread, tteokbokki, fresh strawberry-mochi. A ginkgo-lined kilometre that is the best shopping street in Seoul: independent Korean labels, concept stores, specialty coffee and galleries, with none of the tourist tat of Myeongdong. The interesting stuff is increasingly on the parallel back streets, Serosu-gil, one block either side.

Apgujeong is where Korean hair happens, and it's the one neighbourhood where English-speaking stylists are normal rather than a favour. Expect a long consultation before anyone touches your head, a scalp wash, and a blow-dry-and-style included in the price.
Book ahead and say explicitly that you need an English consultation, the salons below all have English stylists but not every chair does. Bring reference photos; describing a cut in the abstract goes badly in any language. Tipping is not a thing in Korea.

Your booked studio shoot near Gyeongbokgung, hanbok fitting, hair and accessories, then the shoot itself, with edited files delivered afterwards. Allow the full hour; the dressing takes longer than anyone expects.

10:30
Gyeongbokgung Palace: free entry in hanbok Free
The largest of the five Joseon palaces and the throne of the dynasty: Geunjeongjeon hall, the pavilion floating on Gyeonghoeru pond, the restored quarters behind. Free while you're in hanbok. The changing-of-the-guard ceremony at the main gate runs at 10:00 and 14:00.
Go straight to the back half of the grounds first, the crowds cluster at the front third and never make it past the throne hall. The pond pavilion at the northwest corner is the photo everyone wants and it's usually empty before 11.
11:45
Bukchon Hanok Village Free
The hillside of preserved hanok streets between Gyeongbokgung and Changdeokgung, still lived in. Walk up Bukchon-ro 11-gil for the famous roofline-over-city view, then let yourself get lost in the lanes.
People live here and the signs asking for quiet are not decorative, residents have successfully lobbied to close streets to tourists before. Keep your voice down and don't photograph doorways. Hanbok makes you extremely visible; be the good version of that.
lunch: Bukchon / Samcheong-dong, all walkable
Tosokchon Samgyetang (Seochon) ~$18
A whole young chicken stuffed with glutinous rice, ginseng, jujube and garlic, simmered into a milky restorative broth. Seoul's most famous version, in a hanok compound behind the palace. It is enormous, it is pure protein, and it's the correct thing to eat in July, Koreans eat it in the hottest weeks specifically.
Sanchon (Insadong temple cuisine) ~$30
Buddhist temple food as a multi-course set, run by a former monk: a dozen small vegetable, root and tofu dishes in a wooden room. Lighter and stranger than anything else you'll eat this trip. Vegetarian, so pair it with a protein top-up later.
Samcheong-dong kalguksu & mandu houses ~$12
The lane running north from the palace is lined with small noodle-and-dumpling kitchens serving the gallery crowd. Get the steamed mandu alongside the noodles. Quick, and you stay inside the neighbourhood.
Galbitang near Insadong ~$16
Clear beef short-rib soup: a gentler cousin of gomtang, with the ribs falling off the bone into the broth. Understated and very good, and about as protein-dense as lunch gets.
14:00
K-beauty hour: nails, facial or head spa ~$25–70
You've got about 90 minutes here before Yeouido, and this is the cheapest place in the world to spend it well: a proper Korean facial or a gel mani-pedi runs half to a third of US pricing, and the craft is better. Pick one from below rather than trying to stack two; each of these is a real 60–90 minute sit-down, not a quick polish.
⚠️ Almost all of these are reservation-only, Korean nail and skin shops time-block each slot for a custom job and don't take walk-ins. Book 2–3 days ahead. KBeautySeoul (kbeautyseoul.co.kr) and Creatrip both take English bookings for nails, facials, head spa and massage with a foreign card and no Korean phone number, that's the easiest route in. Also: return the hanbok before this, not after.

Korean nail art is the best in the world and roughly half US pricing: ₩35,000 for a clean gel manicure, ₩45,000–70,000 with real art, about ₩70,000 for mani + pedi together.
